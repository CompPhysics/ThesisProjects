## Paper III: Recursive Variational Quantum Compiling (Bilek & Wold)

**Overall.** The core idea is sound and clearly communicated, the recursion in Eqs. (3)–(6) is mathematically consistent for fixed-input-state compiling (step k only ever needs the action on |0⟩, which is exactly what step k−1 guarantees), and the numerical demonstration is a fair controlled comparison. In its current form, though, it reads like a strong workshop paper rather than a journal submission: the benchmark is thin, the related work has not been updated since roughly 2022, and there are enough small technical slips that a referee would likely return it for revision. All are fixable.

**1. A significant inconsistency with the thesis kappa: which cost function?** This is the most important thing I found. Your Chapter 4 presents the *local* fixed-input-state cost, Eq. (4.4), with per-qubit probabilities p₀⁽ⁱ⁾ and figures (4.1, 4.2) showing each qubit measured individually — and it explicitly says "Rather than using only global overlap as the cost function, we use the local fixed-input-state cost." But the paper itself uses the **global** overlap cost throughout: Eq. (2) is 1 − |⟨0|U†V(θ)|0⟩|², and Eq. (7) estimates the all-zero-bitstring probability (the index i there runs over *shots*, not qubits). So the kappa describes a different cost function from the one that produced the paper's numerics. You need to reconcile this: either rewrite kappa §4.1–4.2.4 around the global LET cost (perhaps noting that local variants from Khatri et al. exist and would likely improve gradients further), or state explicitly in the kappa that you present the local variant while the numerics used the global one. As it stands, an examiner comparing the two will conclude one of them is wrong. Relatedly, since global costs suffer ordinary (noise-free) barren plateaus exponentially in n, a "VQC with local cost" baseline in the paper would also strengthen the claim that recursion, rather than cost locality, is doing the work.

**2. Figure 2 caption contains a genuine error.** It states that "V(θ⁽ᵏ⁾) being equal (up to a global phase) to V(θ⁽ᵏ⁻¹⁾)U_k is consistent with measuring the all-zero state." Two problems: the operator order is wrong — the condition is V(θ⁽ᵏ⁾)|0⟩ ≈ U_k V(θ⁽ᵏ⁻¹⁾)|0⟩, with U_k acting *after* the previous ansatz — and FISC only fixes the action on |0⟩, not unitary equality. The caption should say the *states* agree up to phase, in the correct order.

**3. The gradient bound, Eqs. (10) and (13), needs checking against Wang et al.** Eq. (13) is written as an unqualified inequality ∂ⱼC̃ ≤ 2^{(L+K)log₂q} with no prefactor; Wang et al.'s NIBP theorem has explicit prefactors (including system-size dependence), so either quote their bound faithfully or write it as O(·), as you correctly do in the kappa (Eq. 4.6). Also verify the definition q = √(max{|q_X|,|q_Y|,|q_Z|}) in Eq. (10) — the square root looks nonstandard relative to my recollection of Ref. [11], and if it's a deliberate convention it deserves a sentence.

**4. The worked example in §2.3.2 is internally sloppy.** With L = 4096 and L/N = 400, N = 10.24 is not an integer. Use N = 10 (L/N ≈ 410, K ≤ 208) or pick numbers that divide evenly. Also, choosing an ansatz of depth K = 213 to compress subcircuits of depth 400 silently assumes such an ansatz is expressive enough — the example only exercises the gradient bound, and a sentence acknowledging that expressibility is a separate requirement would preempt the obvious objection. The intro's claim that the required depth "is not dependent on the depth of the target circuit" is also loose — it depends through L/N for fixed N; it becomes independent only if N is scaled with L.

**5. The benchmark is thin, and a referee will say so.** Five random circuits, one system size (n = 5), one N (= 5), one ansatz, and ±2σ sample standard deviations over five runs (VQC: 0.34 ± 0.28 — the error bar nearly spans the range). Concrete strengthening, roughly in order of value: (a) an **N-scan** (e.g., N = 2, 5, 10, 20 on the same circuits) — this would directly expose the trade-off between per-step depth and recursive error accumulation that both the paper and the kappa flag as the central open question, and it's cheap at n = 5; (b) more circuits, with the VQC/RVQC comparison reported *paired* per circuit; (c) the local-cost VQC baseline from point 1; (d) a "train noiselessly, execute noisily" reference point, which you nearly have (the ideal-training loss of 0.0038 in Fig. 6) — making it explicit would honestly frame the fact that at 5 qubits everything is classically simulable, so the demonstration is of the mechanism, not of utility. The paper's value proposition kicks in precisely where classical simulation fails, and saying so directly is better than letting the referee say it.

**6. The related work needs a refresh before submission.** The reference list stops around 2022, yet the manuscript is dated May 2026. Your own kappa Chapter 4 already contains the richer, more current positioning — Mizuta et al. (local variational compilation), Kanasugi et al. (Green's functions), and especially Wang et al. on whether error mitigation can rescue trainability [kappa ref. 51], which is directly on-point for §5.1 — none of which the paper cites. Porting the kappa's related-work discussion back into the paper is low-effort and high-value. I'd also note the paper doesn't appear to be on arXiv; posting it (and stating its status in the thesis's list of papers) matters for the committee.

**7. Smaller technical points.** The FakeSantiago appendix (Tables 2–5) is good for reproducibility — but there's no code or data availability statement, which most target venues now expect. Table 1's columns are ordered noisy-then-ideal, opposite to the more logical training-quality-then-execution-quality order you use in kappa Table 4.1. The acronym list sitting after the introduction is unusual; most physics venues would fold it away or drop it.

## Paper IV: Utilizing Small Quantum Computers… (Bilek, single author)

**Overall.** This is the more ambitious and more original of the two, and the Haar/2-design variance calculations in Appendix A are correct — I verified Eq. (20) against the standard second-moment formula, and both the W = I sanity check (result 1) and the non-identity result 1/(d+1) come out right. The internal bookkeeping of the digits model is also consistent (I checked: 7·16 = 112 coefficients and 7·2n_b = 56 expectation values for the full basis; 28/28 for the reduced basis; 240 and 156 total parameters). The main issues are one apparent gap in the covariance argument, a loose counting bound, missing reproducibility details, and a related-work section that has been superseded by your own kappa.

**1. The covariance-vanishing argument in §2.4 appears to have a gap.** You argue Cov[dᵢR_q⁽ⁱ⁾∏B_s⁽ⁱ⁾, dⱼR_q⁽ʲ⁾∏B_s⁽ʲ⁾] = 0 using E[R_q] = 0 and Appendix A.1's E[B_s⁽ⁱ⁾B_s⁽ʲ⁾] = 0. But A.1 only covers subsystems where the two Pauli products have *different* local factors. Consider two products i ≠ j that **differ only on the differentiated subsystem q** and share identical factors on every s ≠ q: then every factor in ∏_{s≠q}E[B_s⁽ⁱ⁾B_s⁽ʲ⁾] equals E[(B_s⁽ⁱ⁾)²] ≠ 0, and the covariance reduces to E[dᵢd*ⱼ]·E[R_q⁽ⁱ⁾R_q⁽ʲ⁾]·(nonzero). In the supervised case you're rescued if the dᵢ are independently drawn with zero mean, so E[dᵢd*ⱼ] = 0. In the **VQE case, however, the dᵢ are deterministic functions of the shared cᵢ** (Eq. 16), and cross-moments like E[cos²(c)sin²(c)] = 1/8 are nonzero, so E[dᵢd*ⱼ] ≠ 0 in general. The argument then rests entirely on E[R_q⁽ⁱ⁾R_q⁽ʲ⁾] vanishing for two *different* local observables under the same random U_q — which is never shown. Either prove that second moment vanishes under the 2-design assumption, or restrict the statement to feature sets excluding such pairs, or handle the term explicitly. This is exactly the kind of thing a careful referee will find, and it's better that you find it first. (The numerical Fig. 6 evidence is unaffected — this is about the generality of the analytical claim.)

**2. The 2^{2K} counting bound is loose by a square — in your favor.** Conjugating a Pauli term sequentially, e^{−icP}M e^{icP} = cos(2c)M − i sin(2c)PM when {P, M} = 0, so each branching rotation at most *doubles* the number of distinct Pauli strings; K branchings give ≤ 2^K distinct strings, not 2^{2K}. Your 2^{2K} comes from expanding V† and V independently before recombining, which over-counts duplicate strings. The worked example in §3.1.3 would then read "at most 2³ = 8 expectation values per Hamiltonian term" rather than 64. Since the tighter bound strengthens your own scaling claims (n^β instead of n^{2β} in §2.3.2), this is worth incorporating — just make sure the E[dᵢd*ᵢ] bookkeeping in §2.4.2, which currently indexes 2^{2K} (possibly duplicate) coefficients, is adjusted consistently or explicitly kept as an over-count.

**3. Reproducibility gaps in the TFIM experiment.** As far as I can find, **the values of J and h are never stated anywhere** — not in §3.1.1, not in Table 1, not in the Fig. 4 caption. Since the difficulty of the TFIM ground state depends strongly on h/J (trivial deep in either phase, hardest near criticality), this omission materially affects how impressive "0.1% relative error" is; if you ran at or near h/J = 1, say so prominently. Related inconsistencies: the conclusion claims n = **2**, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, 16 spins while §3.1.3 and §4.1 list n = 4, …, 16 (no n = 2); and the Fig. 4 caption uses three symbols for two quantities — "(E_VQE − E₀)/E₀ between the ground state energy E₀ and our approximation E_G". Pick one symbol, and consider |E − E₀|/|E₀|.
   Also a plain typo in §3.1.3: the second worked example writes "a measurement of X₈ will result in V†**X₃**V = P̄₆†P̄₅†P̄₄† X₈ P̄₄P̄₅P̄₆" — the left-hand side should be V†X₈V.

**4. The classical cost of feature selection is not analyzed.** §2.2.2 correctly shows the number of *local* expectation values is polynomial, but then proposes selecting Pauli-product features by gradient or expectation-value magnitude. The candidate *products* number up to 4ⁿ, so "evaluate all possible expectation values and combine the largest-magnitude ones" hides a potentially exponential classical search unless done greedily per subsystem (top-t factors per subsystem still gives t^S combinations). A short paragraph specifying the actual selection algorithm and its classical complexity would close this hole — right now the polynomial-scaling headline claim quietly depends on it.

**5. Statistics and noise.** Fig. 6's variance estimates come from only 20 random initializations with no error bars; the variance of a variance estimate at that sample size is large, and the "fluctuations due to different parameter initializations" you mention are partly just estimator noise. Bootstrap error bars, or 100+ samples, would make the flat trend convincing. On the ML side, the paper is honest that "only one instance of each model **have** been trained" (→ has), but the abstract's "generalize to out-of-sample data with 100% accuracy" oversells a 91-sample test set on the easiest digit pair; the kappa's more modest phrasing ("both models learned the task") is the right register — adopt it in the paper. Finally, the products-of-noisy-estimates issue (multiplicative error amplification) that your own kappa §5.1.8 identifies as a key limitation is absent from the paper's conclusion; even a paragraph with the leading-order variance of a product of independent estimators would preempt the referee.

**6. Related work needs the same refresh as Paper III — and here your kappa has already done the work.** The paper cites Bravyi, Fujii (Deep VQE), Marshall, and Tüysüz, but not entanglement forging (Eddins et al.), not CutQC, and — most importantly given how you position the method in the thesis — not the operator-backpropagation line (Fuller et al., npj QI 2026; the circuit-cutting combination, arXiv:2510.19467). Since your v4 predates Fuller et al., an updated version engaging with that work would both strengthen the priority narrative (your observable-transformation step anticipates theirs) and demonstrate awareness. Chapter 5 of the kappa contains essentially the finished text for this; port it back. Also fix the intro's duplicated citation: "Quantum Support Vector Machines [3], Quantum Principal Component Analysis [4], Quantum Nearest-Neighbor Classification [5] **and Quantum Principal Component Analysis [4]**" — QPCA appears twice.
